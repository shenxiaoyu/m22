FMP: Neckio

In my FMP, I designed, developed and evaluated a smart system intended to prevent neck pain by encouraging regular exercise during work hours. The system was conceived based on literature research and an extensive user survey. The design decisions were backed up by professional guidance from my client, Prof. dr. Annick Timmermans, who is an expert in rehabilitation science and physiotherapy. After going through an iterative design process with formative testing, I came to the final design that aims at providing an interactive exercises experience and better integrating the system into people’s daily routine in a minimally obstructive way. System user experience and users’ intrinsic motivation were evaluated positively in a short-term user evaluation.

So far, this project received positive feedback. I have been invited to pitching my work at Eindhoven Maker Faire 2016 by a communication advisor, who considered my work as fresh and inspiring. Besides, a lecturer from Homeschool Utrecht whose expertise lies in chronic care, also expressed her appreciation of Neckio and showed interest to cooperate in the future research.

This project was taken as an opportunity for an overall evaluation of my abilities as a qualified designer. I acted as a project designer and a manager at the same time to manage the project with a clear plan and vigorous execution ability. During the project, I developed my skills in electronics prototyping and software programming by making the workable tracking device and application, which I had little experience before. Visual styles were iteratively explored, as well as the product design of tracking device, for visually aesthetic perception, better wearability and ease of use. To create a better user experience, I actively involved users, client, experts in design process and inspired by co-creation methods. Given the interim feedback, I tried hard to improve my writing skills by reading related books and writing a paper for my m12 project. The outcome of this learning activity is my final report, which is written in the form of a scientific paper.

Electives


To Make is To Grasp: from 2D to 3D

Various techniques aiming at exploring and refining form were introduced in this elective, such as sketching with pencil, pastels, fine liner and markers, foam modeling, Photoshop and Illustrator. By applying these techniques to an original design, an all-in-one printer, lessons were learned about describing the aesthetic aspects of a design proposal. Design techniques should be chosen appropriately for different design case. During my final design task, I found it is hard to imagine an organic style by sketching on paper only, so a little piece of foam was used as 3D sketching to explore the shape. By improving the resolution step by step, the prototype acted as the demonstrator of my design concepts in communication and idea sharing. The challenge here is how to avoid “very complete” deliverables but put more emphasis on the essential aspects or details in the design which elicits beauty in the experience. Furthermore, balancing the aesthetic and practical aspects of the product. My design was regarded as a sculpture more than a product in the design process. It was suggested to pay more attention to human-machine interaction details.

Useful Links: Report

Design for Focused and Peripheral Interaction

This course explored how to build on the way people effortlessly divide their attention over several everyday activities by designing interactive products and systems. My learning objectives of this elective is to gain an overview of attention theories and learn practical guidelines of peripheral interactions design and evaluation. This course started with an exploration of human attention abilities in both theory and practice, where the final design BUX was grounded. BUX is a controller for Philips Hue Light to interact with the lighting ambience at home in an effortless way. The process towards the final outcome came with frequent design iterations where potential peripheral factors were identified. As lecturer mentioned, ease of use doesn’t mean the interaction is peripheral. To tell whether an interaction is peripheral depends on the mental resource demand. Therefore, evaluating the designs with users in a stimulative context is necessary to get feedback and make further design decisions.
